01

Kota Kinabalu

Arrival

The South China Sea glittered from the taxi window. Kota Kinabalu, seen from the air, is a city that knows exactly where it stands — a Malaysian port town pressed between open water and a solid green wall of mountain. Jetlagged and overheating, we dropped our bags, found cold Milo, and walked to the nearest waterfront. The next morning we were on a boat to Sapi Island, masks fogging, following sea turtles through water too blue to be trusted. For one whole day, we let the mountain wait.

02

Day Three · 1,500m

The Drive to
Kundasang

The road out of KK winds upward through palm oil and dense rainforest until the air suddenly cools and everything shifts to a different shade of green. Kundasang sits at 1,500 metres — a highland valley that grows strawberries and vegetables for half of Sabah. We pulled off at Sosodikon Hill to watch the evening light drag long shadows across the valley floor. The mountain wasn't visible yet. It hid behind its own cloud. We ate at a hawker stall, the kind where the tables wobble and the laksa is perfect, and went to bed early. Tomorrow we would register at the park.

The highlands

At 1,500 metres the air smells different — a coolness cut with woodsmoke and wet earth. It's highland Southeast Asia: strawberry farms and warung stalls and school buses on mountain roads. The kind of place that slows you down before you realise it's happened.

Before the climb

The day before the climb we rode ATVs through red mud, visited a cattle ranch where the cows were indifferent to us in a deeply reassuring way, and ate strawberries still warm from the plant. It felt important to fill up on ordinary pleasures while we still could.

The mountain wasn’t visible yet —
it hid behind its own cloud.

Day Four · Eve of the Climb

We registered at the park office and paid our fees in a hall where the walls were covered in summit permits going back decades. That evening the mountain appeared briefly at dusk — the whole silhouette visible for one clear moment before the cloud closed back in. We ate breakfast food for dinner and were in bed by eight.

03

4,095m · Low’s Peak

The Summit

The alarm rang at 2 AM. In the headlamp dark the trail became the whole world: the next wooden step, the next fixed rope, the next breath of thinning air. At 3,500 metres my lungs began filing formal complaints. The cold was the sort that doesn’t feel cold at first — just precise, as if calibrated. By the final push across the granite moonscape to Low’s Peak, every step was a negotiation. And then, just as dawn split open over the South China Sea, there was nowhere left to climb.

Low’s Peak

The highest point between the Himalayas and New Guinea. I sat on cold granite and didn’t say anything for a long time. The cloud layer below looked like a second ocean, impossibly still. We had earned every centimetre of that view with every blister.

Above the clouds

The summit plateau is raw granite — no soil, no vegetation, just polished rock in the half-light. Before the sun rises it looks like another planet. After it rises, it looks like a painting of another planet. Either way, you feel very small and very awake.

There was nowhere left to climb —
4,095 metres of nowhere.

04

Via Ferrata

The Wall

Six hours after reaching the summit, I was back on the mountain — this time clipped to a steel cable bolted into the granite face. The Via Ferrata descends the sheer east wall of the summit plateau, and it turns out via ferrata is Italian for “suspended above a cloud with shaking legs.” Our guide moved with the easy confidence of someone who has done this four hundred times. I moved with the concentrated focus of someone doing it exactly once.

The descent

The valley appeared below through gaps in the mist, impossibly far down. You clip your carabiner to the next bolt, find the next foothold, breathe. One section at a time. The mountain lets you down slowly, as if reluctant to be done with you.

The valley below,
impossibly far — impossibly beautiful.
