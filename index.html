KINABALU

A journey to the roof of Borneo — 4,095 metres above the South China Sea.

Scroll
01

27 May

Islands, then
the long road up

The morning belonged to the sea. We island-hopped across Tunku Abdul Rahman Marine Park — snorkelling the coral off Manukan, then Sapi, then letting the afternoon dissolve into a slow beach day. Lunch by Jesselton Port, dinner shoulder-to-shoulder at the Lido Square hawker stalls, and then the city fell behind us. Two hours of dark highway later we rolled into Hidden Hill in Kundasang just before 9pm — the air cooler now, the mountain somewhere out there in the black.

02

28 May

Cool air,
open hills

Kundasang mornings are made for slow starts. We spent ours at Desa Dairy Farm — a walk through the green, a few activities, and lunch at a spot our driver swore by (he was right). The afternoon got louder: ATVs tearing up the trails at Borneo Adventure Sport and the scramble up Sosodikon Hill next door. We closed the day at Anooh Coffee, mountain views on one side and the day’s dust still on our boots.

Desa Dairy Farm

A wide fold of highland valley, green slopes, a few cows, and cooler air than anywhere else we’d been in Sabah. The kind of morning that doesn’t feel like it costs anything — until you’re back in the car and an hour has gone by.

Trails and hills

The afternoon picked up pace. ATVs through red mud at Borneo Adventure Sport, then the scramble up Sosodikon Hill for the view across the valley. Anooh Coffee as the light went orange, the mountain somewhere just above the clouds.

Mountain views on one side —
the day’s dust still on our boots.

03

29 May

The calm before
the climb

One last easy day before the mountain. Breakfast at Second Breakfast Club, then a wander through the rows at Sabah Tea Garden with lunch at the plantation restaurant. A grocery run to Bataras, a dinner we cooked ourselves back at the homestay, and lights out before ten. We knew what was coming.

04

30 May

Up to Pendant
Hut

The shuttle dropped us at Timpohon Gate at 7:30, and by 9 we were climbing. Six kilometres of relentless ascent, a packed lunch eaten somewhere on the way up, the forest thinning as the air did the same. We reached Laban Rata, took our via ferrata briefing at Pendant Hut by 3, ate early, and tried to sleep — knowing 2am would come fast.

Pendant Hut

Laban Rata is a different altitude entirely — air noticeably thinner, the canopy long gone. We took the via ferrata briefing at Pendant Hut, ate our meal early, and tried to sleep while the temperature dropped outside. 2am would come fast.

Above the cloud

By the time we reached Laban Rata the cloud layer had dropped below us. Looking back down the trail, the valley floor was gone. Looking up, the granite slabs rose into a sky that was already beginning to darken.

Tried to sleep —
knowing 2am would come fast.

05

31 May

Low’s Peak —
4,095m

Up at 2am, head torches cutting the dark, the final push to the top. We reached Low’s Peak — 4,095 metres — just as the sun broke the horizon. Then the part nobody quite prepares you for: seven hours on Walk the Torq, the world’s highest via ferrata, hands and boots against bare granite. We collected our bags from Pendant Hut, caught a short, hard-earned breather, and made the full descent to Timpohon Gate, finishing around 10pm — wrecked, in the best possible way.

Walk the Torq

Seven hours on the world’s highest via ferrata — hands and boots against bare granite, the valley below measuring itself in cloud-layers. We collected our bags from Pendant Hut and kept moving. Timpohon Gate by 10pm, wrecked, in the best possible way.

The valley below,
impossibly far — impossibly beautiful.
